Observation-Driven Second-by-Second Seismic-Intensity Forecasting

A low-false-alarm complement to source-parameter earthquake early warning in Taiwan.

Da-Yi Chen1,*  &  Yu-Hsuan Chang1
¹ Central Weather Administration, Taipei, Taiwan · Corresponding author: Da-Yi Chen
Earthquake early warning Seismic intensity Transformer PLUM eBEAR Taiwan
Abstract

Earthquake early warning (EEW) systems commonly estimate origin time, hypocenter, and magnitude before converting those source parameters into expected shaking — an architecture that provides rapid regional coverage, but whose errors in early association, location, magnitude, or rupture representation can propagate into alert decisions. We evaluate an observation-driven complement that forecasts final station-level seismic intensity directly from incomplete one-second intensity sequences. The Second-by-second Intensity Transformer Forecaster (SITF) uses no hypocenter, magnitude, source model, or ground-motion prediction equation as model input. Trained on 198 Taiwan earthquakes and evaluated on 161 nonoverlapping earthquakes (97,354 station-event samples per early window), SITF achieved 91.5% precision at 20 s with a 0.38% false-alarm rate at the national public-warning threshold of intensity ≥ 4, and 96.4% precision, 82.5% probability of detection, and an F1 of 0.889 at 40 s. SITF is best interpreted as a low-false-alarm, observation-driven confirmation layer that complements — rather than replaces — conventional source-parameter EEW and direct observation-propagation methods.

A layered warning architecture

Three pathways, three different jobs

The scientific claim is not that SITF dominates — it occupies a distinct, low-false-alarm operating point that complements the other two pathways.

Source parameters

eBEAR

Taiwan's operational Earthworm-based system: waveform acquisition, P-wave picking, association, rapid source estimation, and dissemination. Fastest broad-area alert, but exposed to early-solution error.

Observation propagation

PLUM-like

Estimates shaking at a target from strong motion already observed at neighboring stations. Robust to source-model error; favors high detection at the cost of more false alarms (FAR 9.8–13.9%).

Observation forecasting

SITF (this work)

Forecasts a station's own eventual intensity from its incomplete one-second sequence — no source model. Favors reliable, station-specific confirmation with FAR consistently below 0.4%.

Model architecture & training

A compact temporal Transformer

A temporal convolutional embedding feeds a Transformer encoder; pooled encoded frames drive a 10-class ordered-intensity head and a continuous-intensity regression head.

Limitations

What this result does not yet show

  1. 1 The target is final peak intensity, not a fixed future horizon (t+h); a production model for t+1/t+3/t+5/t+10s remains future work.
  2. 2 The analysis is retrospective and origin-time referenced — true public lead time and dissemination latency are not measured.
  3. 3 Strong-intensity samples are sparse, and class imbalance can make station-level reliability unstable.
  4. 4 The model is station-local and does not yet exploit the spatial coherence of a dense network.
  5. 5 The eBEAR comparison is event-level sensitivity, not a matched station-level benchmark.
  6. 6 The 2024 Hualien earthquake is not yet quantified in the independent test set and should be treated as illustrative only.
